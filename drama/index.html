The Map

The Drama Triangle

Developed by Stephen Karpman, the Drama Triangle describes three psychological roles people adopt to avoid feeling their own pain: Rescuer, Victim, and Perpetrator.

This is not about real-world situations where people are legitimate victims of harm, where helping is genuinely needed, or where someone is perpetrating abuse. These are psychological positions adopted to outsource responsibility for one's own internal experience. A psychological victim can create a perpetrator as surely as a rescuer can create a victim.
The Three Roles

How Each Avoids Pain

Rescuer

AVOIDS OWN PAIN BY FIXING SOMEONE ELSE'S
  • Blames the situation / world / perpetrator for not being right
  • Gets to feel useful, needed, superior while avoiding their own vulnerability
  • First choice: rescue the victim from the perpetrator
  • Second choice: slide to victim and blame the perpetrator for "making" the situation unfixable
"Let me help you"
→ so I don't have to feel my own helplessness

Victim

AVOIDS OWN PAIN BY BLAMING IT ON SOMEONE ELSE
  • Blames the perpetrator for their pain; attaches to the rescuer for false safety
  • Gets to avoid agency and responsibility while staying in familiar suffering
  • First choice: blame the perpetrator for how they feel
  • Second choice: cling to the rescuer to feel temporarily safe/saved
"This is happening to me"
→ so I don't have to feel my power to change it

Perpetrator

AVOIDS OWN PAIN BY TAKING IT OUT ON SOMEONE ELSE
  • Blames the victim for making them feel/act this way; attacks the rescuer for defending the victim
  • Gets to discharge pain outward through harm instead of feeling it inward
  • First choice: abuse / control / harm the victim
  • Second choice: attack the rescuer, sliding them into victim position
"You made me do this"
→ so I don't have to feel the hurt underneath my rage

All three roles keep you from feeling what's actually yours to feel. The triangle is a closed system — and the roles constantly rotate. The dance continues until someone stops avoiding and feels their own experience directly.

I
Level One

Transformation

Getting out of the drama.

1

Accept current reality completely

Transformation of the Rescuer
  • Stop trying to fix, manage, or improve the situation
  • Let go of needing things to be different so you can feel okay
  • Face the feelings you've been avoiding by staying busy trying to fix problems
2

Experience your capacity for radical, instantaneous change

Transformation of the Perpetrator
  • Know that radical shifts are possible right now, especially interpersonally
  • Stop taking your pain out on others
  • Reclaim the energy you've been using to control/harm and redirect it toward self-responsibility
3

Reclaim agency over your internal state

Transformation of the Victim
  • Stop waiting for the perpetrator to change or the rescuer to save you
  • Your internal experience is 100% your responsibility to feel and process
  • Separate what happened from who has to do the work — yes, they hurt you and it's your job to metabolize this pain
  • Feel what you've been avoiding by giving your power away to external circumstances
II
Level Two

Enjoyment

Living beyond the drama.

1

Enjoy current reality exactly as it is

Not just accept — actively find pleasure in what's here. The rescuer tried to change reality to feel safe; now you enjoy it as is.

2

Enjoy the electricity of potential change

Especially in relational and interpersonal moments. The perpetrator forced change through harm; now you enjoy the aliveness of possibility.

3

Enjoy your internal experience, whatever it is

No matter what you think it should be. The victim blamed others for how they felt; now you enjoy feeling whatever you feel.

The dance continues until someone stops avoiding
and feels their own experience directly.
